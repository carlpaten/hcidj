Feedback by the Evaluation Team

We received mostly positive feedback about our testing plan and documentation. The evaluation team reported that the user manual introduced all needed concepts to the a user with no previous DJing experience in 10 minutes. There was some confusion expressed by user when choosing and loading a song to either deck. The user did in fact complete the task; however, she hesitated and made some mistakes. The user failed to complete one of the tasks, loading an effect, without needing assistance. That was mainly due to the fact system was not entirely completed, which meant no visual feedback in terms of LEDs and some non-functioning buttons.

The results of their heuristic analysis according to the guidelines with which we provided them showed that the design was fairly simple considering the amount of features involved, that functional groups on the controller were laid out clearly and were easy to locate, and that physical controls were obvious, for instance, what to push and what to slide. Nevertheless, they suggested that the controls would still be confusing to a user that is not familiar with DJing concepts. In addition, the evaluation team criticized that the design provided no error prevention mechanisms. That, however, is somewhat unfeasible, given that our design is an interface to a DJ system. It is similar to playing a musical instrument: the instrument does not stop the user from playing bad music.

We received very positive feedback on our user-centred design which, while maintaining many important features, was largely simplified to accommodate the target audience, amateurs. On the other hand, the evaluation team reported that some DJ notions, such as having two play decks, were rather ambiguous for new users. Consequently, the evaluation team recommended adding explanations of DJing concepts and instructions to mastering DJing skills in the form of reference manuals or video instructions.

The strengths and weaknesses of our design as identified by the evaluation team can be summarized as:

Strengths:

  • Functional groups were split and placed in a logical manner.
  • Controls were easy to locate.
  • The design was fairly simplified considering the amount of functionality it provided.
  • The user manual introduced all basic concepts needed to operate the controller.

Weaknesses:

  • No error prevention provided by the interface.
  • Little instructions about DJing notions and skills.
  • Unclear workflow for loading effects.

In response to the feedback of the evaluation team, we decided the following:

  • Jogging strip: We decided not to follow the evaluation team’s advice of making it vertical. The main reason is because our goal was not to simulate the usual scratching movement, but rather to make the movement the most intuitive possible: in most musical players such as smart phones and computers, in order to fast-forward through a song one needs to do a left-right movement (move the song cursor left or right). Therefore we concluded that the most intuitive movement for moving forward-backward is left-right.

  • Cue mix slider: We updated the user manual to explain what it does and avoid confusion.

  • Music Library: We increased space between the folder and song icons and their knobs, and we made the lines thicker to make the icons more visible, as suggested by the evaluation team. We also changed “LOAD” to “LOAD SONG” to make the feature clearer.

Revised Prototype 

The major changes that we made since the last prototype are the following: changed units layout, added labels, added LEDs.

Layout Changes:

  • We switched the positions of the library unit and the volume & crossfader unit: the library is the feature that will be the most used, so having it closer to the user requires a lot less movement and effort.
  • We switched the positions of the FX Unit and the Play/Beats Unit: same idea. The play button is the most used after the library, so having it closer to the user instead of having them in the top left and top right corners is much better.
  • FX Unit: We put the FX Select subunit on top, because it’s probably the feature that will be the least used, so it makes sense for it to be further from the user.
  • We added the Tempo Unit (Sync & Master): originally we wanted to implement this functionality automatically in the software but we figured out that it is not possible. It is crucial for the user to be able to synchronize the 2 tracks and therefore a necessary feature.

Labels:

We added the following labels to our design, as suggested by the TA in the Computer Prototype feedback:

  • Added Min-Max lines for all knobs in FX Unit
  • Added “ON” for blue enable buttons in FX Unit
  • Changed “Select” to “FX Select” in FX Unit
  • Changed “lo-mid-hi” in EQ Unit to “bass-mid-treble”: this is more descriptive in terms of frequency and more familiar
  • Changed “Load” to “Load Song” in Library Unit

LEDs:

We believe that a lot of the failed tasks were due to lack of visual feedback. This is one major feature that we added in the alpha system. When something is on it will be immediately visible, helping with every single functionality of the system.

User Manual

All the tasks that failed due to lack of understanding of how DJing works (what effects mean, what cueing is, what filtering is, etc.) were fixed by adding more explanations and examples in the user manual (they are not things we can fix by changing the design).
